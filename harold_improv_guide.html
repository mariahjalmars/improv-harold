Beat 3

Each scene set (A, B, C) explores a different angle of the opening's theme. By Beat 3 the worlds start colliding. ~25–30 min total.

Mapping & Beat Transitions

Mapping is how you track every scene's game, characters, and theme so you know where to go next. Think of it as building a web in your head while watching from the back wall.

You're always asking: What is this scene's game? Who are these characters? What theme from the opening does this connect to? When you know that, you can call back, heighten, or merge at the right moment.

How Beats Evolve

1
Beat 1 — Establish. Set the world, the characters, and find the game. Don't rush the funny. Lay the foundation. The audience needs to understand who these people are and what's unusual here.
2
Beat 2 — Heighten. Return to the SAME characters in a different situation, but the game escalates. The unusual thing is now MORE so. Don't introduce a new game — deepen the existing one. Ask: "If this were true in Beat 1, what's MORE true now?"
3
Beat 3 — Converge & pay off. Worlds can bleed into each other. Characters from Scene A can meet Scene C's world. Call-backs land here. The audience has been tracking everything — reward them. This is where tag-outs, merges, and emotional resolution live.
B1 → B2

Beat 1 to Beat 2

You return to the same characters, but time has passed or situation shifted. The game from Beat 1 should be recognisable — just escalated one clear step.

Ask yourself: what does the game WANT to become? Follow that logic.

Don't reset. Don't explain. Just more of the same unusual thing — louder, clearer, weirder.
B2 → B3

Beat 2 to Beat 3

The Harold starts to collapse in on itself — in a good way. Themes rhyme. Characters cross paths. A line from Scene A might echo in Scene C.

No need to force convergence — if you've tracked well, it happens naturally.

Your job here: be a good editor. Know when a scene is done. Trust the map you've built in your head.

Practical mapping tip: While watching from the back wall, mentally label each scene: "A is about control. B is about not belonging. C is about being seen." Now you know what themes rhyme. When B's character walks into C's world — that's your convergence moment.

Mapping isn't a system — it's active listening with intent. The better you map, the better your choices for re-entries, tag-outs, and call-backs.

Glossary

Cards marked CLASS TERM may be specific to your teacher or school — not official UCB manual terms.

Game of the Scene

The unusual thing that happens + a pattern of making it more and more itself. Find it, name it internally, then heighten it.

Half Game

When only ONE person is playing the game while the other plays it straight. Often funnier than both being weird.

Character Game

Game driven by who the person IS — attitude, worldview, want. The character's logic IS the game.

Monologue

A real story from life, told simply. The group mines it for themes, relationships, and games to use in the Harold.

Group Game

The whole ensemble explores one idea together, often abstractly. No scene, just theme.

Heighten

"Yes, and" the game — each beat takes the unusual thing ONE step further. Don't reset, don't explain. Just more.

Voice of Reason

One player reacts "normally" to the absurdity. Creates contrast. Without it, everything is chaos and nothing lands.

Tag-out

Someone runs in, taps a player out, takes their place — shifting time, location, or relationship to explore the game further.

Call-back

Revisiting a character, phrase, or idea from earlier in the Harold. The audience remembers. Make it pay off.

Mapping

Tracking each scene's game, characters, and theme from the back wall so you know when and how to re-enter, call-back, or converge.

Object Work

Physically committing to props/space that aren't there. Grounds scenes in reality and feeds character.

First Unusual Thing

The moment something odd appears. Stop. Name it. That's probably your game. Build from there.

If This Is True, What Else Is True?

The core UCB question for playing the game. Once you find the unusual thing, use this to explore its logic and heighten it beat by beat.

Consequence

Making the events of a scene actually matter. If the game has no stakes or fallout, it stays flat. Raise the cost of what's happening — for the characters, not just the audience.

Comedic Tension

The gap between what the audience can see and what the character refuses to acknowledge. The longer you hold it without breaking, the funnier it gets. Don't resolve it too early.

Full Analogy

When a character says something is "like X" — play it fully as if it IS X. Don't half-commit. Taking the comparison all the way is where the comedy lives.

Twisted Logic

A character justifying their unusual behaviour through reasoning that makes perfect sense to them — but to no one else. The internal logic is consistent; the worldview is just bent.

Pop a Wheelie

Demonstrating your character's game or trait clearly for the audience — showing what your deal is. Don't be subtle about it. If you've got a game, pop it.

Class Term

Game Battle

When two players are each trying to play different games in the same scene — pulling in opposite directions. A note to get on the same page and agree on one game together.

Class Term

Living Room

"The Living Room" is also a longform format inspired by the Harold. As a scene term, it refers to a grounded, domestic setting used to establish base reality before the game emerges.

Class Term

Riders Room

A backstage or offstage space used in rehearsal context — often where the ensemble waits, watches, and maps before entering. Staying sharp in the riders room means tracking everything.

Cut To

An edit that jumps the scene forward or backward in time — e.g. "cut to: one year later" or "cut to: the night before." Used to explore the game in a different moment without losing the thread.

Class Term

Workshop an Idea

In rehearsal: pausing a scene to explore a moment together before continuing. Not done in performance — it's a rehearsal tool to find what a game could become before committing.

Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do

  • Make your scene partner look good
  • Find the game early, then heighten it
  • Have a clear point of view as your character
  • Be specific — "my Ford Fiesta" not "my car"
  • React truthfully before being clever
  • Map scenes from the back wall actively
  • Use the monologue — mine it for real themes
  • Trust your scene partner's choices
  • Let silence breathe
  • Call back — audiences LOVE memory
  • Keep the voice of reason grounded
  • Return to the same game in Beat 2, escalated
  • Let consequences land — don't skip past them
  • Hold comedic tension as long as you dare
  • Pop a wheelie — show us your game clearly

✗ Don't

  • Block (deny what your partner established)
  • Ask questions to get out of commitment
  • Chase laughs at the expense of the scene
  • Introduce new games in Beat 2 or 3
  • Explain the joke or the game out loud
  • Play multiple games at once — game battle
  • Make your partner wrong
  • Switch off when watching from the back
  • Drift — keep the game consistent in tone
  • Be random instead of unusual
  • Force convergence in Beat 3 — trust the map
  • Run away from the weird thing
  • Half-commit to an analogy — go all the way
  • Resolve tension too early for comfort

Tips & Tricks

Find the gameThe first unusual, specific, weird thing — name it. Build from it. Don't wait.
If/then thinking"If this is true — what else is true?" Explore the logic of the game rather than escaping it.
CROWCharacter · Relationship · Objective · Where. Establish these fast. Scenes that drag often lack one.
Commit to emotionCharacters with strong feelings create friction and game naturally. Don't be neutral.
Match, then leadIn group games: mirror what others set up, then add one thing. Don't overpower, don't just copy.
Rule of threesGame needs ~3 beats to land. Establish → Confirm → Audience expects it → subvert or pay off.
Map activelyWhile watching: label each scene by theme. "A=control, B=belonging, C=visibility." That's your convergence roadmap.
Tag-outs are giftsTag out to explore a different angle of the same game — not to fix or escape.
Monologue miningListen for real emotion, odd detail, contradiction — not the funny line. That's your material.
Voice of reason trickWhen everything is crazy, being completely normal IS the comedy. Underreact.
Beat 2 questionAsk yourself: "What does this game want to become?" Follow that. Don't reinvent.
Twisted logicStay inside your character's warped worldview. The more consistent the logic, the funnier the behaviour.
Hold the tensionDon't let characters off the hook too soon. Comedic tension is a rubber band — stretch it before you snap it.
Your edgeYou read rooms well — trust that. Your facilitation instinct for when a group needs energy vs. space? That's gold in ensemble improv.
