The Harold

UCB Improv · Quick Reference · Maja's Edition

Harold Structure

Opening
Group Game / Monologue-based opening
Find the themes. Mine for gold.
Beat 1
Scene A1
Scene B1
Scene C1
Group 1
Group Game #1 — explore theme together
Beat 2
Scene A2
Scene B2
Scene C2
Group 2
Group Game #2 — raise the stakes
Beat 3
Scenes converge / call-backs / tag-outs

Each scene set (A, B, C) explores a different angle of the opening's theme. By Beat 3 the worlds start colliding. The Harold is ~25–30 min total.

Key Terms

Game of the Scene

The unusual thing that happens + a pattern of making it more and more itself. Find it, name it internally, then heighten it.

Half Game

When only ONE person is playing the game (reacting unusually) while the other plays it straight. Often funnier than both being weird.

Character Game

Game driven by who the person IS (attitude, worldview, want) rather than the situation. The character's logic IS the game.

Monologue

A real story from life, told simply. The group mines it for themes, relationships, and games to use in the Harold.

Group Game

The whole ensemble explores one idea/game together, often in a more abstract or physical way. No scene, just theme.

Heighten

"Yes, and" the game — each beat should take the unusual thing ONE step further. Don't reset, don't explain. Just more.

Voice of Reason

One player reacts "normally" to the absurdity. Creates contrast. Without it, everything is chaos and nothing lands.

Tag-out

Someone runs in, taps a player out, and takes their place — often shifting time, location, or relationship to explore the game further.

Call-back

Revisiting a character, phrase, or idea from earlier in the Harold. The audience remembers. Make it pay off.

Object Work

Physically committing to props/space that aren't there. Grounds scenes in reality and feeds character.

Initiation

The first line or action that starts a scene. Best initiations contain a who, what, and a point of view baked in.

First Unusual Thing

The moment something odd appears in a scene. Stop. Name it. That's probably your game. Build from there.

Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do

  • Make your scene partner look good
  • Find the game early, then heighten it
  • Have a clear point of view as your character
  • Be specific — "my Ford Fiesta" not "my car"
  • React truthfully before being clever
  • Support group games with commitment
  • Use the monologue — mine it for real themes
  • Trust your scene partner's choices
  • Let silence breathe
  • Call back — audiences LOVE memory
  • Keep the voice of reason grounded
  • Play the game the same way each beat, then escalate

✗ Don't

  • Block (deny what your partner established)
  • Ask questions to get out of commitment
  • Chase laughs at the expense of the scene
  • Introduce new games in beat 2 or 3
  • Explain the joke or the game out loud
  • Play multiple games at once
  • Make your partner wrong
  • Check out when you're not in a scene
  • Drift — keep the game consistent in tone
  • Be random instead of unusual
  • Make it about yourself instead of the ensemble
  • Run away from the weird thing — run toward it

Tips & Tricks

Find the gameWhat is the first unusual, specific, or weird thing? Name it. Build from it. That's your game — don't wait.
If/then thinking"If this is true in this world — what else is true?" Explore the logic of the game rather than escaping it.
CROWCharacter · Relationship · Objective · Where. Establish these fast. Scenes that drag often lack one of these.
Commit to emotionDon't be neutral. Characters with strong feelings create friction and game naturally.
Match, then leadIn group games: mirror what others set up, then add one thing. Don't overpower, don't just copy.
The rule of threesGame needs ~3 beats to land. First: establish. Second: confirm. Third: audience knows what to expect — then you can subvert or pay off.
Watching from the backYour job offstage is to track themes and games so you know when/how to re-enter. Never switch off.
Tag-outs are giftsTag out to explore a DIFFERENT angle of the same game — not to fix, escape, or take over.
Monologue miningListen for the real emotion, the odd detail, the contradiction — not the funny line. That's your material.
Voice of reason trickWhen everything is crazy, being completely normal IS the comedy. Underreact. It creates contrast.
Improv mantra"Yes, and" is the foundation — but "yes, and" the GAME, not just the information.
Personal tip for youYour improv background in facilitation is gold — you read rooms well. Trust that instinct to feel when the group needs energy vs. space.
