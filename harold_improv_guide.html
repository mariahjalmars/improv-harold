Improv Guide

UCB · The Harold · Maja's Edition

What is the Harold?

The Harold is a long-form improv structure — roughly 25–30 minutes — performed by an ensemble of 6–9 people. It starts with a single suggestion from the audience, which gets explored through three beats of scenes and two group games. By the end, all the threads connect.

The Harold is not about plot. It's about finding a game — the unusual thing that makes a scene funny — and then heightening it across three beats while weaving it together with everything else happening in the show.

The Opening

The Harold begins with a suggestion — one word from the audience. The ensemble then does a group opening: a shared, often abstract exploration of that suggestion. It could be a pattern game, a monologue, a physical exploration, or a mix. The goal is not to be funny — it's to generate raw material: themes, images, emotions, and relationships that can feed the scenes to come.

Think of the opening like tuning an instrument together before a concert. You're agreeing on the key without spelling it out. The best openings feel like one mind thinking, not six people performing.

What to listen for in an opening: real emotion, contradiction, a specific image, a surprising relationship. Not the funniest line — the most resonant one. That's what you'll build scenes from.

1
Beat 1 — Establish
Set the world · Find the game · Don't rush

Three separate two-person scenes launch from the opening. Each scene explores a different angle of the theme — different characters, different worlds, different games. They are not connected to each other yet. That happens later.

Your job in Beat 1 is simple: find the first unusual thing and start playing it. Establish who these people are, what their relationship is, and what the strange thing is. Don't rush to be funny. Let the game emerge from the reality.

The audience needs to understand the base reality before the game lands. A doctor's office is only funny when we believe it's a doctor's office first.

Find the first unusual thing Establish base reality first Commit to your characters Don't rush to weird Don't play multiple games
G
Group Game — Deepen the Theme
Whole ensemble · Abstract · No scene structure

After the three Beat 1 scenes, the full ensemble comes together for a group game. This is not a scene — it's a shared exploration of the Harold's theme, often more physical, abstract, or musical than a regular scene.

Group games let the ensemble breathe together, find the emotional centre of the show, and signal to the audience: this is what this Harold is really about. Heighten quickly — group games don't get second beats, so build fast and commit fully.

Build fast — no second beat Match and then lead Don't make it a scene Don't overpower — listen first
2
Beat 2 — Heighten
Same characters · Same game · More so

The three scenes return — same characters from Beat 1, but in a new situation. The game from Beat 1 is still the game. Your only job is to make it more. The unusual thing is now undeniable. The stakes are higher. The logic is more twisted.

Ask yourself: "If this was true in Beat 1, what's MORE true now?" Don't reset and don't introduce a new game. You are deepening what already exists, not starting over. The audience has been tracking these characters — reward that investment.

A common mistake: playing Beat 2 like Beat 1, just louder. That's not heightening — that's repeating. Something must actually shift. The game escalates; the consequence is real; the character is further committed to their warped worldview.

Same game, escalated Let consequences land Follow the twisted logic Don't introduce new games Don't reset the scene Don't just repeat Beat 1 louder
G
Group Game — Raise the Stakes
Push further · More committed · Edge of control

The second group game goes further than the first. The ensemble now understands the Harold's emotional core and the group game should reflect that — more committed, more specific, closer to the edge.

This is the last moment of shared breath before the Harold collapses inward. Use it to set the emotional temperature for Beat 3.

3
Beat 3 — Converge & Pay Off
Worlds collide · Call-backs · Shorter scenes

Beat 3 is where the Harold starts to fold in on itself. Scenes are shorter. Characters from different storylines can meet. A line from Scene A might echo in Scene C. Call-backs land here — the audience has been tracking everything and now gets rewarded.

You don't need to force convergence. If you've mapped well and played the games honestly, it happens naturally. The themes were always connected — Beat 3 just makes that visible.

Tag-outs and edits are powerful here. Use them to cross the streams — bring a character into another scene's world and watch the games collide.

Trust the map — don't force it Use call-backs Let scenes be short Cross the worlds Don't start new games Don't over-explain connections

Mapping — How to Track It All

Mapping is what you do from the back wall while you're not in a scene. You're actively tracking every scene's game, characters, and emotional theme — building a mental web so you know when and how to re-enter, call-back, or converge.

Think of yourself as both a performer and an editor. The editor in you watches the whole Harold and spots the connections. The performer in you acts on them.

Practical mapping habit: As each scene plays out, mentally label it by theme — not by plot. "A is about needing to be seen. B is about faking competence. C is about loyalty." Now you can see what rhymes. When B's character stumbles into C's world — that's your convergence.

Mapping isn't a system. It's active listening with intent. The better you map, the more inevitable your Beat 3 feels.

Beat Transitions

B1 → B2

Beat 1 to Beat 2

Return to the same characters, shifted situation. The game is recognisable — just escalated one clear step.

Ask: What does this game WANT to become? Follow that logic.

Don't reset. Don't explain. Just more of the same unusual thing — clearer, weirder, higher stakes.
B2 → B3

Beat 2 to Beat 3

The Harold folds inward. Themes rhyme. Characters cross paths. A line from Scene A echoes in Scene C.

No need to force it — if you've mapped well, it happens naturally.

Be a good editor. Know when a scene is done. Trust the map you built from the back wall.

Glossary

Cards marked CLASS TERM may be specific to your teacher or school.

Game of the Scene

The unusual thing that happens + a pattern of making it more and more itself. Find it, name it internally, then heighten it.

Half Game

When only ONE person is playing the game while the other plays it straight. Often funnier than both being weird.

Character Game

Game driven by who the person IS — attitude, worldview, want. The character's logic IS the game.

Monologue

A real story from life, told simply. The group mines it for themes, relationships, and games to use in the Harold.

Group Game

The whole ensemble explores one idea together, often abstractly. No scene structure — just shared theme.

Heighten

"Yes, and" the game — each beat takes the unusual thing ONE step further. Don't reset, don't explain. Just more.

Voice of Reason

One player reacts "normally" to the absurdity. Creates contrast. Without it, everything is chaos and nothing lands.

Tag-out

Someone runs in, taps a player out, takes their place — shifting time, location, or relationship to explore the game further.

Call-back

Revisiting a character, phrase, or idea from earlier in the Harold. The audience remembers. Make it pay off.

Mapping

Tracking each scene's game, characters, and theme from the back wall so you know when and how to re-enter, call-back, or converge.

Object Work

Physically committing to props/space that aren't there. Grounds scenes in reality and feeds character.

First Unusual Thing

The moment something odd appears. Stop. Name it. That's probably your game. Build from there.

If This Is True, What Else Is True?

The core UCB question for playing the game. Once you find the unusual thing, use this to explore its logic and heighten it beat by beat.

Consequence

Making the events of a scene actually matter. Raise the cost of what's happening — for the characters, not just the audience.

Comedic Tension

The gap between what the audience can see and what the character refuses to acknowledge. Hold it as long as you dare — don't resolve it too early.

Full Analogy

When a character says something is "like X" — play it fully as if it IS X. Taking the comparison all the way is where the comedy lives.

Twisted Logic

A character justifying their unusual behaviour through reasoning that makes sense to them but no one else. Consistent internal logic, bent worldview.

Pop a Wheelie

Demonstrating your character's game or trait clearly for the audience. Don't be subtle about it. If you've got a game, show it.

Class Term

Game Battle

When two players are each pulling toward different games in the same scene. A note to agree on one game and play it together.

Class Term

Living Room

A grounded, domestic-feeling scene used to establish base reality before the game emerges. Also a longform format inspired by the Harold.

Class Term

Riders Room

The offstage space where the ensemble watches, maps, and waits. Staying sharp in the riders room means actively tracking everything happening onstage.

Cut To

An edit jumping the scene forward or backward in time — e.g. "cut to: one year later." Used to explore the game in a different moment without losing the thread.

Class Term

Workshop an Idea

In rehearsal: pausing to explore a moment together before continuing. A tool to find what a game could become. Never done in performance.

Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do

  • Make your scene partner look good
  • Find the game early, then heighten it
  • Have a clear point of view as your character
  • Be specific — "my Ford Fiesta" not "my car"
  • React truthfully before being clever
  • Map scenes from the back wall actively
  • Use the monologue — mine it for real themes
  • Trust your scene partner's choices
  • Let silence breathe
  • Call back — audiences LOVE memory
  • Keep the voice of reason grounded
  • Return to the same game in Beat 2, escalated
  • Let consequences land — don't skip past them
  • Hold comedic tension as long as you dare
  • Pop a wheelie — show us your game clearly

✗ Don't

  • Block (deny what your partner established)
  • Ask questions to get out of commitment
  • Chase laughs at the expense of the scene
  • Introduce new games in Beat 2 or 3
  • Explain the joke or the game out loud
  • Play multiple games at once — game battle
  • Make your partner wrong
  • Switch off when watching from the back
  • Drift — keep the game consistent in tone
  • Be random instead of unusual
  • Force convergence in Beat 3 — trust the map
  • Run away from the weird thing
  • Half-commit to an analogy — go all the way
  • Resolve tension too early for comfort
