What is the Harold?

The Harold is a long-form improv structure — roughly 25–30 minutes — performed by an ensemble of 6–9 people. It starts with a single suggestion from the audience, which gets explored through three beats of scenes and two group games. By the end, all the threads connect.

The Harold is not about plot or story. It's about finding a game — the unusual thing that makes a scene funny — and then heightening it across three beats while the whole ensemble weaves everything together.

The Opening

The Harold begins with a suggestion — one word from the audience. The ensemble does a group opening: a shared, often abstract exploration of that suggestion. The goal is not to be funny — it's to generate raw material: themes, images, emotions, relationships that can feed the scenes to come.

Think of the opening like tuning an instrument together before a concert. You're agreeing on the key without spelling it out. The best openings feel like one mind thinking, not six people performing.

What to listen for: real emotion, contradiction, a specific image, a surprising relationship. Not the funniest line — the most resonant one. That's your raw material for scenes.

1
Beat 1 — Establish
Set the world · Find the game · Don't rush

Three separate two-person scenes launch from the opening. Each explores a different angle of the theme — different characters, different worlds. They are not connected to each other yet. That happens later.

Your job: find the first unusual thing and start playing it. Establish who these people are, what their relationship is, and what is strange. Build to absurdity — earn it. Don't leap straight to weird. The audience needs to believe the base reality before the game lands.

Beat 1 is the calmest, most grounded beat. Longer than the others. Take your time. Strong characters here mean you have something worth calling back in Beat 2 and 3.

Find the first unusual thing Establish base reality first Build strong characters Don't rush to weird Don't play multiple games Don't tag out in Beat 1
G
Group Game — Deepen the Theme
Whole ensemble · Abstract · No scene structure

After Beat 1, the full ensemble comes together for a group game. This is not a scene — it's a shared, often physical or abstract exploration of the Harold's theme. Heighten quickly — group games don't get second beats, so build fast and commit fully.

This is the ensemble breathing together. The best group games make the audience feel the emotional centre of the show without spelling it out.

Build fast — no second beat Match and then lead Don't make it a scene Don't overpower — listen first
2
Beat 2 — Heighten
Same game · Raised stakes · Shorter scenes

Beat 2 returns to the games from Beat 1 — but not necessarily the same characters or setting. The game is heightened: the unusual thing is now more extreme, the stakes are higher, the consequences are real.

Beat 2 scenes are typically shorter than Beat 1. Much of the groundwork is already done — now you just need to escalate. Ask: "If this was true in Beat 1, what's MORE true now?"

A common mistake is playing Beat 2 like Beat 1, just louder. That's repeating, not heightening. Something must actually shift. The game escalates. The consequence lands. The character is further committed to their warped worldview.

Escalate the game Let consequences land Follow the twisted logic Don't introduce new games Don't just repeat Beat 1 louder Don't focus on plot

Wait — What Actually Is Beat 2? (Plain Language)

🟢 Beginner Reality Check

Beat 2 is NOT "the same scene, continued."

This is one of the most common mistakes beginners make in improv — and completely normal at this stage. You had two people in a scene with a funny dynamic. Beat 2 comes around and you put the same two people back on stage, pick up roughly where you left off, and make it a bit weirder.

That feels right. It isn't.

What's missing: the game. The game is not the characters. It's not the situation. It's the weird pattern underneath. Two scenes can have totally different characters and settings and still be the same game — that's the whole point.

Think of it like a song cover. Different artist, different arrangement — but you recognise the melody. The melody is the game. Beat 2 is the cover version.

The Classic Beginner Mistake — And How To Fix It

❌ What most beginners do

Beat 1: Two friends at a café. One keeps giving unsolicited life advice. The other gets increasingly annoyed. It's funny. You find the game.

Beat 2: Same two people. Still at the café (or somewhere nearby). Still giving advice. Maybe now it's about something bigger. A bit weirder. More dialogue.

The audience thinks: "Oh right, these two again." But the game hasn't moved. It's just more of the same scene.

Why it happens: nobody on the back wall asked "what is the actual game here?" so Beat 2 had nothing to build from. This is a mapping problem, not a performance problem.

✅ What should happen

Beat 1: Same scene. One friend gives unsolicited life advice. Game identified from back wall: "Person who thinks they know better than everyone, even when it's not asked for."

Beat 2 (Time Dash): Same character — now at a hospital, giving unsolicited surgical advice to a doctor mid-operation. Higher stakes. Same game. Still totally unqualified and totally certain.

OR Beat 2 (Analogous): New characters entirely — a co-pilot giving unsolicited landing advice to a senior pilot. Same game (unwanted certainty), new world, new performers.

The audience thinks: "Oh! That's the same thing — but bigger." That's the laugh.

🟢 The one question to ask yourself from the back wall

While watching a Beat 1 scene, don't track the plot. Ask yourself:

"What kind of person is this? What do they always do?"

Not: "they argued about coffee" — but: "person who can't admit they're wrong."

Not: "they were at work" — but: "person who treats every tiny thing like a life-or-death crisis."

That label is your game. Write it in your head. That's all mapping is. When Beat 2 comes, ask: "Where else does this kind of person show up — but in a bigger, higher-stakes version?" That's your initiation.

The Three Types of Beat 2

When you initiate a Beat 2 scene, you have three options. All three are valid — what matters is that you're serving the game, not the plot.

Time Dash

Take the same character(s) from Beat 1 and see them in a new situation — usually further forward in time. The character's game continues, just in a higher-stakes context.

Beat 1: A hostess gets promoted to head chef despite having no cooking skills.

Time Dash Beat 2: Same hostess — now she's been put in charge of a Michelin-star kitchen. Same game (wildly unqualified person refusing to admit it), higher stakes.

Analogous

Strip away the specifics of Beat 1 and find the core game. Then play that game in a completely new world, with new characters. Often done by different performers than Beat 1.

Beat 1: Overbearing parent micromanages child at piano recital.

Analogous Beat 2: Overbearing pilot micromanages co-pilot while landing a plane. Same game (control freak + higher stakes situation), totally new people and world.

Tangential

A loosely connected idea sparked by Beat 1 — same theme, but explores a different angle. Often saved for Beat 3. Good for when you flagged something interesting but didn't have room to play it.

Beat 1: Two siblings competing for their parent's approval.

Tangential Beat 3: Two employees competing for praise from a boss who doesn't notice either of them. Same emotional theme — being unseen — different world entirely.

Mapping tip for Beat 2: While watching Beat 1 from the back wall, ask yourself: "What is the game here, stripped of the details?" Label it in your own words — not "hostess who can't cook" but "person in way over their head who won't admit it." That label is what you carry into Beat 2. That's your initiation.

Worked Example — Full Harold Trace

Suggestion: "Vacation". Follow Scene A through the whole Harold to see how mapping and beating work in practice.

Scene A — Traced Through All Three Beats

SUGGESTION: VACATION · THEME THAT EMERGED: Escape from responsibility

Opening
What you mine
Group opening on "Vacation"
The ensemble free-associates: sunburn, leaving your phone at home, a dad who can't stop checking email, lying on a beach pretending to be fine, a kid who just wants to go home.
→ You notice: the dad checking email feels loaded. Someone in the ensemble physically can't stop moving even when lying still. That's your raw material — people who can't let go of control even when they're supposed to.
Beat 1
Establish
Scene A1 — The Hotel Room
Game: Person on holiday who can't stop working
Two colleagues are on a company retreat in Mallorca. One (A) is lying by the pool reading a novel. The other (B) has their laptop out, is on a conference call, and keeps insisting everything is fine — they're "totally relaxed, this is just a quick check-in."
The first unusual thing: B is visibly stressed and working, but insisting they're on holiday. A starts pointing this out. The game emerges: B keeps finding increasingly absurd reasons why what they're doing "doesn't count" as work.
→ From the back wall you note: Game = person who redefines reality to avoid admitting they can't let go. The twisted logic is the game.
Mapping
Back wall
What you're tracking from the back wall
Scene A: Can't stop working / redefines reality
Scene B: Family pretending to have fun / performing happiness
Scene C: Tour guide making everything sound fine when it isn't
→ You see the theme: people pretending to be OK when they're not. That's what rhymes across A, B, and C. Now you know what Beat 3 convergence looks like.
Beat 2
Heighten
Option 1: Time Dash — Same characters, new situation
Same game, higher stakes
Same B, now at their own wedding. They're the one getting married. But there's a work crisis and they keep sneaking off to take calls, insisting "this is fine, I'm present, this is just logistics." Same character, same twisted logic, now applied to the most personal moment of their life.
Option 2: Analogous — New characters, same game
A surgeon in the operating theatre who insists this counts as their "me time." Different person entirely — but the game (redefining reality to avoid admitting they can't let go) is identical. Higher stakes: they're operating on someone.
→ Both are valid. Time dash keeps the character. Analogous raises stakes faster. Pick what serves the Harold.
→ Common mistake: playing the same scene again with slightly more stress. That's repeating, not heightening. The game must escalate, not just the volume.
Beat 3
Converge
Worlds collide
The workaholic from Scene A ends up on the same tour from Scene C — still on a call, still insisting they're "100% present." The tour guide (who was also pretending everything was fine) starts narrating the workaholic's breakdown as part of the tour. The family from Scene B wanders past, also visibly not fine, waving and smiling.
All three games — redefining reality, performing happiness, narrating denial — collide in one scene. The audience has been tracking all of it. The payoff feels earned.
→ You didn't plan this. You mapped it. Because you labelled each scene's game from the back wall, you knew which worlds rhymed. Beat 3 just made the connections visible.

Mapping — Active Listening with Intent

Mapping is what you do from the back wall while you're not in a scene. You're actively tracking every scene's game, characters, and emotional theme — building a mental web so you know when and how to re-enter, call-back, or converge in Beat 3.

Think of yourself as both a performer and an editor. The editor in you watches the whole Harold and spots the connections. The performer acts on them at the right moment.

The mapping habit: As each scene plays, label it by theme — not plot. Not "two people at a restaurant" but "someone being responsible for something they broke." Strip out the specifics. What's left is the game. That label is what you carry forward.

When three scenes rhyme thematically, Beat 3 writes itself. You don't force convergence — you just know the doors that are open.

Beat Transitions

B1 → B2

Beat 1 to Beat 2

Strip the specifics. Label the game. Initiate Beat 2 from that label — not from the plot, not from "what happened next."

Ask: What does this game WANT to become? Time dash, analogous, or tangential — pick what serves the game.

Don't timedash with plot. Think "if this, then what" — not "what happened next."
B2 → B3

Beat 2 to Beat 3

The Harold folds inward. Themes rhyme. Characters cross paths. Your mapped labels tell you which scenes connect.

Scenes are short now — 30 seconds each. Don't start new games. Just heighten and converge.

Be a good editor. Know when a scene is done. Trust the map you built from the back wall.
G
Group Game 2 — Raise the Stakes
Push further · More committed · Edge of control

The second group game goes further than the first. The ensemble now understands the Harold's emotional core — the group game should reflect that. More committed, more specific, closer to the edge. This sets the temperature for Beat 3.

3
Beat 3 — Converge & Pay Off
Worlds collide · Call-backs · Very short scenes

Beat 3 is where the Harold folds in on itself. Scenes are very short — 30 seconds or less each. Characters from different storylines meet. Games collide. Call-backs land here — the audience has been tracking everything and now gets rewarded for it.

You don't need to force convergence. If you've mapped well and played the games honestly, it happens naturally. The themes were always connected — Beat 3 just makes that visible.

Trust the map — don't force it Use call-backs Keep scenes very short Cross the worlds Don't start new games Don't over-explain connections

Glossary

Cards marked CLASS TERM may be specific to your teacher or school.

Game of the Scene

The unusual thing that happens + a pattern of making it more and more itself. Find it, name it internally, then heighten it.

Half Game

When only ONE person is playing the game while the other plays it straight. Often funnier than both being weird.

Character Game

Game driven by who the person IS — attitude, worldview, want. The character's logic IS the game.

Monologue

A real story from life, told simply. The group mines it for themes, relationships, and games to use in the Harold.

Group Game

The whole ensemble explores one idea together, often abstractly. No scene structure — just shared theme.

Heighten

"Yes, and" the game — each beat takes the unusual thing ONE step further. Don't reset, don't explain. Just more.

Voice of Reason

One player reacts "normally" to the absurdity. Creates contrast. Without it, everything is chaos and nothing lands.

Tag-out

Someone runs in, taps a player out, takes their place — shifting time, location, or relationship to explore the game further.

Call-back

Revisiting a character, phrase, or idea from earlier in the Harold. The audience remembers. Make it pay off.

Mapping

Tracking each scene's game, characters, and theme from the back wall so you know when and how to re-enter, call-back, or converge.

Time Dash

A type of Beat 2 where you take the same character(s) forward in time to a new, higher-stakes situation. The character's game continues — the context escalates.

Analogous Beat

A type of Beat 2 where you strip the specifics and replay the same game with entirely new characters and world. Often played by different performers.

Object Work

Physically committing to props/space that aren't there. Grounds scenes in reality and feeds character.

First Unusual Thing

The moment something odd appears. Stop. Name it. That's probably your game. Build from there.

If This Is True, What Else Is True?

The core UCB question for playing the game. Once you find the unusual thing, use this to explore its logic and heighten it beat by beat.

Consequence

Making the events of a scene actually matter. Raise the cost of what's happening — for the characters, not just the audience.

Comedic Tension

The gap between what the audience can see and what the character refuses to acknowledge. Hold it as long as you dare — don't resolve it too early.

Full Analogy

When a character says something is "like X" — play it fully as if it IS X. Taking the comparison all the way is where the comedy lives.

Twisted Logic

A character justifying their unusual behaviour through reasoning that makes sense to them but no one else. Consistent internal logic, bent worldview.

Pop a Wheelie

Demonstrating your character's game or trait clearly for the audience. Don't be subtle about it. If you've got a game, show it.

Class Term

Game Battle

When two players are each pulling toward different games in the same scene. A note to agree on one game and play it together.

Class Term

Living Room

A grounded, domestic-feeling scene used to establish base reality before the game emerges. Also a longform format inspired by the Harold.

Class Term

Riders Room

The offstage space where the ensemble watches, maps, and waits. Staying sharp in the riders room means actively tracking everything happening onstage.

Cut To

An edit jumping the scene forward or backward in time. Used to explore the game in a different moment without losing the thread.

Class Term

Workshop an Idea

In rehearsal: pausing to explore a moment together before continuing. A tool to find what a game could become. Never done in performance.

Do's & Don'ts

✓ Do

  • Make your scene partner look good
  • Find the game early, then heighten it
  • Have a clear point of view as your character
  • Be specific — "my Ford Fiesta" not "my car"
  • React truthfully before being clever
  • Map scenes from the back wall actively
  • Label each scene's game in your head (not plot)
  • Use the monologue — mine it for real themes
  • Trust your scene partner's choices
  • Let silence breathe
  • Call back — audiences LOVE memory
  • Keep the voice of reason grounded
  • Return to the same game in Beat 2, escalated
  • Let consequences land — don't skip past them
  • Hold comedic tension as long as you dare
  • Pop a wheelie — show us your game clearly

✗ Don't

  • Block (deny what your partner established)
  • Ask questions to get out of commitment
  • Chase laughs at the expense of the scene
  • Introduce new games in Beat 2 or 3
  • Explain the joke or the game out loud
  • Play multiple games at once — game battle
  • Make your partner wrong
  • Switch off when watching from the back
  • Time dash with plot — think "if this, then what"
  • Play Beat 2 like Beat 1 just louder
  • Force convergence in Beat 3 — trust the map
  • Run away from the weird thing
  • Half-commit to an analogy — go all the way
  • Resolve tension too early for comfort
  • Tag out in Beat 1 — you don't know the game yet
